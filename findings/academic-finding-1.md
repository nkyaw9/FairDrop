# Academic Paper Analysis — Ticket Bots: The Effect of Online Ticket Sales On the Entertainment Business

Source: https://soar.suny.edu/server/api/core/bitstreams/a179ba37-fafd-47c1-93e6-85861a82f720/content
Brett Rozell, *Ticket Bots: The Effect of Online Ticket Sales On the Entertainment Business* (2020)

---

# Key Research Findings

## 1. Ticket Scalping Existed Before The Internet

The paper explains that ticket scalping has existed since the 1800s and was already a known issue long before online ticket sales. The internet did not create the problem, but drastically increased its scale and accessibility.

### Insight
The core problem is not simply resale itself, but the technological advantage and automation that modern systems introduced.

---

## 2. Bots Are The Main Force Multiplier

Automated ticket buying software allows scalpers to:
- Open hundreds of purchase windows simultaneously
- Bypass queues
- Buy tickets within seconds
- Scale operations across thousands of accounts

The paper references large-scale bot operations making millions of dollars from automated ticket purchasing.

### Insight
Bots and automation create an unfair speed advantage over regular users and dramatically increase reseller buying power.

---

## 3. Current Anti-Bot Systems Still Struggle

The paper discusses examples such as:
- Hamilton ticket sales
- Hannah Montana tour sales
- Ticketmaster queue systems

Even with:
- phone verification
- waiting rooms
- purchase limits
- queue systems

bots were still able to dominate ticket sales.

### Insight
Current systems appear reactive rather than fully preventative. Advanced reseller systems continue adapting faster than existing protections.

---

## 4. Transparency Is A Major User Trust Problem

Consumers often:
- Do not understand how queues are organized
- Do not know why tickets disappear instantly
- Feel pressured into panic buying
- Suspect bots or faster users have unfair advantages

### Insight
The fairness problem is not only technical. It is also psychological and trust-based.

---

## 5. Resale Markets Exist Because Demand Exceeds Supply

The paper argues that resale markets suggest tickets may initially be priced below market equilibrium. When demand greatly exceeds supply, resale markets naturally emerge.

### Insight
The ticketing problem is deeply connected to economics, scarcity, and consumer willingness to pay — not just bots alone.

---

## 6. Scalpers Are Economically Complex

The paper presents a nuanced perspective:
- Scalpers help guarantee sellouts
- Secondary markets provide ticket liquidity
- Some consumers rely on resale markets to access events later

However, large-scale automated scalping creates unfairness when real fans lose access entirely.

### Insight
The issue may not be resale itself, but unfair acquisition methods and technological abuse.

---

## 7. Current Platforms Already Use Anti-Bot Systems

The paper describes systems currently used by platforms such as Ticketmaster:
- Smart Queue systems
- Waiting rooms
- Phone verification
- Purchase limits
- Interactive seat maps

### Insight
The opportunity may not be inventing anti-bot systems from scratch, but improving:
- transparency
- fairness
- trust
- abuse detection
- queue visibility

---

## 8. Legal Solutions Alone Are Insufficient

Although the BOTS Act made automated ticket purchasing illegal federally, the paper argues that:
- bot systems continue evolving
- black markets still exist
- financial incentives remain extremely high

### Insight
Technical, behavioral, and policy solutions may all be necessary together.

---

## 9. Consumer Behavior Still Supports Resale Markets

Survey findings from the paper showed:
- Most users primarily purchase tickets online
- Many users are still willing to pay above face value for sold-out events

### Insight
High emotional demand causes consumers to tolerate inflated pricing and unfair systems.

---

# Overall Research Themes

Recurring themes identified throughout the paper:
- Queue distrust
- Lack of transparency
- Panic buying
- Emotional urgency
- Bot dominance
- Multi-account abuse
- Reseller frustration
- Supply and demand imbalance
- Platform incentive misalignment
- Scalability limitations
- Fairness vs free-market tradeoffs

---

# Final Insight

This paper suggests that the modern ticketing problem is not simply caused by resellers alone. The deeper issue involves automation, transparency, incentives, trust, and the imbalance between regular users and highly optimized reseller systems.

The findings support the idea that future solutions may need to focus on fairness infrastructure, transparent queue systems, anti-abuse detection, and trust-centered user experiences rather than only eliminating resale markets entirely.
